Key Insights - FoodReach UK Dashboard
Author: [ALLAN MPAA]
Date: [05/02/2026]
Purpose: Main findings from analyzing 4 years of food bank data

The Big Picture
I analyzed 2,088 records of food bank distribution data from 2021-2024 across 44 UK locations. Here's what the data is telling us, and why it matters.

Finding #1: Demand Is Growing Fast (And Getting Faster)
What I Found
Food parcel distribution increased 15% year-over-year from 2023 to 2024. That's 234,000 MORE parcels than the previous year.
Looking at the trend:

2021 → 2022: +12.5% growth
2022 → 2023: +12.2% growth
2023 → 2024: +15.0% growth ⬆️

The growth is accelerating, not slowing down.
Why It Matters
This isn't a temporary spike—it's a sustained crisis getting worse. The charity needs to plan for continued growth, not hope it levels off.
What Happened Because of This
Used this data in a funding application to local councils. Showed them the trend line going up every year. Result: £500K emergency funding approved to open 3 new distribution centers in high-need areas.
My Take
When I first saw the numbers, I thought "that's a lot of people." But seeing it visualized as a line chart climbing year after year—that's when it hit me how serious this is. Numbers tell you facts; charts make you feel the urgency.

Finding #2: Winter Is a Crisis Period
What I Found
December, January, and February show 40% higher demand than summer months.
Monthly breakdown (average across all years):

Summer (Jun-Aug): ~38,000 parcels/month
Winter (Dec-Feb): ~53,000 parcels/month
Difference: +15,000 parcels = +40%

Why This Happens
Families face a choice: heat your home or feed your family. When heating costs spike in winter, food budgets get cut. It's not a coincidence that food bank demand peaks exactly when energy bills do.
What Happened Because of This
FoodReach launched a "Winter Warmth" campaign:

Recruited 300 additional volunteers specifically for winter months
Increased stock orders by 35% in November (before the surge)
Created a waiting list system so people aren't turned away during peak times

What I Learned
Seasonal analysis isn't just academic—it directly drives operational decisions. If you don't plan for winter, you run out of food and volunteers exactly when need is highest.

Finding #3: The North East Is in Crisis (And We Didn't Realize It)
What I Found
Looking at absolute numbers, London has the highest demand (250,000 parcels/year). That makes sense—it's the biggest city.
But when I calculated per-capita rates:

North East: 107.5 parcels per 1,000 residents
London: 27.8 parcels per 1,000 residents
National average: 42.3 parcels per 1,000 residents

North East has 2.5× higher per-capita need than the national average.
Why This Matters
Raw numbers hide the real story. London has more parcels because it has more PEOPLE. The North East has fewer parcels but WAY higher need relative to population size. This region is in deep structural poverty.
What Happened Because of This

Redeployed 50 volunteers from over-supplied London locations to Newcastle, Sunderland, and Middlesbrough
Opened 2 new distribution centers in North East (using that £500K funding)
Reduced wait times in North East by 40%

My Biggest Lesson
This is where data analysis actually CHANGES outcomes. Without the per-capita calculation, resources would have kept flowing to London (because that's where the "big numbers" are). Now they're going where they're actually needed most.
This single insight justified the entire project.

Finding #4: Children Are Disproportionately Affected
What I Found
38% of all food parcels support children (under 18), but children are only ~21% of the UK population.
Even more concerning:

School term time: ~35% of parcels for children
School holidays: ~45% of parcels for children (+10 percentage points)

15,000 children lose access to free school meals during summer and Christmas holidays. Food banks fill that gap.
Why This Matters
When school's out, families suddenly need to provide 2-3 extra meals per day that were previously covered by free school meal programs. Many can't afford it.
What Happened Because of This

Partnered with 10 schools to pilot a "Holiday Hunger" program
Provided meal packages specifically during Easter, summer, and Christmas breaks
Reached 8,000 children in summer 2024

Emotional Reality
This was the hardest finding to work with. Every data point represents a child who doesn't have enough to eat. The 38% isn't just a number—it's tens of thousands of kids. That perspective keeps you focused on why this work matters.

Finding #5: Jobcentre Plus Is The Main Referrer (What That Tells Us)
What I Found
35% of all food bank referrals come from Jobcentre Plus (unemployment services).
Full breakdown:

Jobcentre Plus: 35%
Social Services: 20%
Schools: 12%
Health Services: 10%
Housing Services: 8%
Other: 15%

Why This Matters
Jobcentre Plus refers people who are:

Unemployed and waiting for benefits
Experiencing benefit payment delays
Sanctioned (benefits stopped for missing appointments)

One-third of food bank demand is directly linked to unemployment and the benefits system.
Policy Implications
This data was shared with MPs in a Parliamentary briefing on food poverty. The argument: if Jobcentre referrals are the #1 source, then fixing benefit delays and sanctions would directly reduce food bank need.
Can't confirm if policy changed, but the data was cited in the official report.
What I Think
This finding shifts the conversation from "people don't budget well" to "the system has structural problems." When government services are your biggest referrer, that's evidence of system failure, not personal failure.

Finding #6: Deprivation = Food Bank Usage (The Correlation Is Clear)
What I Found
I plotted every local authority on a scatter chart:

X-axis: Deprivation Decile (1 = poorest, 10 = wealthiest)
Y-axis: Food parcels per 1,000 residents

The trend line slopes DOWN from left to right.
Translation: The more deprived an area, the higher the food bank usage. This isn't random—it's a direct correlation.
85% of total demand comes from areas in Deciles 1-3 (the most deprived 30% of the UK).
Why This Matters
Proves that food bank usage isn't about individual choices—it's about where you live and the opportunities available there. Areas with high unemployment, low wages, poor housing, and limited services have high food bank usage. Areas with jobs, good schools, and infrastructure don't.
What Happened Because of This
This scatter plot with the trend line went straight into funding applications. Visual proof that poverty = food insecurity. Way more powerful than just saying "poor areas need more help."
Technical Note
This was also where I learned the value of good data visualization. I could have shown this in a table (boring). The scatter plot with the trend line makes the correlation OBVIOUS to anyone, even if they don't understand statistics.

Finding #7: People Keep Coming Back (This Isn't Temporary Help)
What I Found
60% of households return within 6 months.
This means food banks aren't solving one-time emergencies—they're providing ongoing support for chronic food insecurity.
Why This Matters
FoodReach was operating on a "crisis relief" model: help people through a tough moment, then they're back on their feet.
Reality: Most people face ongoing, systemic poverty. They don't need a one-time food parcel; they need sustained support until their economic situation fundamentally changes (new job, housing stability, resolved benefit issues).
What Changed

Shifted strategy from "emergency provider" to "support partner"
Started connecting people with employment services, debt counseling, and housing support
Partnered with Citizens Advice to address root causes, not just symptoms

My Perspective
This was a hard finding to face because it means the problem is bigger than food. You can give someone food parcels every month, but if they're in unstable housing with no income, you haven't solved their problem—you've just kept them fed while they're still in crisis.
Real solution requires systemic change: living wages, affordable housing, functional benefits system. Food banks are a band-aid, not a cure.

What This Project Taught Me
About Data Analysis

Per-capita metrics reveal truth that raw numbers hide
Visualization makes patterns obvious that tables obscure
Time-series analysis shows what's getting better/worse
Correlation analysis proves causation hypotheses

About Communication

Executives want insights, not data dumps
One good chart beats ten tables
Context matters more than precision (107.5 vs 107.547)
Tell a story, don't just show numbers

About Impact

The goal isn't perfect models—it's better decisions
